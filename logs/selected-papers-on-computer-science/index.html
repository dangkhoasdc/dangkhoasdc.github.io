In my opinion, this is the most accessible book from Don Knuth. However, some chapters are still heavy on mathematical stuff, i.e., Chapter 2 - even though it is very interesting and covers many aspects of algorithms and data structures).

Chapter 0

There are many interesting quotes in the chapter. Some of them are worth to mention: > Computer science answers the question “What can be automated?”

Some principal subfields come from the early era of computer science: - The analysis of algorithms: What Knuth do. - Software engineering: The study of languages and methodologies for programming, of OS for controlling computer resources, and of utility programs tailored to significant applications - Computer graphics and visualizations: The development of tools for analysis and synthesis of images - Computer architecture and communication: The design of machines and of networks - Artificial intelligence: the development of tools for accumulating, applying, and reasoning about knowledge. - Human-Computer Interaction: the study of interfaces between people and computers. - Robotics: the development of mobile machines with sensors. - And interdisciplinary connections with other branch of science, technology, medicine, and the humanities.

Chapter 1: the relationship between CS and Mathematics.

The chapter reminds me the note of Prof <…> about the status of studying mathematics of computer scientists. Overall, the chapter is very interesting and instructive. However, I totally lost at the nearly end of the text when Knuth discussed some advanced analysis of hashing. It is too complicated to understand.

Chapter 2: Combinatorial problems in CS

Surprisingly, the chapter is very attracting. It covers many problems along with many algorithms and data structures.

Chapter 3: Algorithms.

One of the heart of the book. I just love it. The chapter introduces many famous problems in Computer Science. From the simple problem, i.e., searching with linear data representation (array), the author discuss more complex algorithms such as hashing, balanced binary trees. Chapter 2 and 3 are absolutely fantastic.
